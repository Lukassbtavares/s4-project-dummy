01 / Orientation

Rebuilding a target that survives the hit.

Earlier student teams at HAN proved a low-cost vulnerable-road-user dummy was feasible — but the joints were fragile and the bicycle couldn't reliably hold the dummy up. This project re-engineers both.

Advanced Driver Assistance Systems rely on accurately detecting vulnerable road users — cyclists and pedestrians. To validate them safely and repeatedly, physical dummy targets stand in for real people during testing.

Commercial VRU dummies are expensive and not built to survive multiple impacts, making them impractical for a student or research setting. This project delivers an accessible alternative.

Work runs under two linked sub-projects: Project 279, a static cyclist configuration, and Project 276 (CORDS-VTS), a dynamic dummy that mimics realistic pedestrian and cyclist motion.

The goal across both: a system that is modular, lightweight, sensor-detectable, and robust enough for repeated use in ADAS testing scenarios.

03 / The Dummy

A simplified human, built to be replaced.

Not anatomically detailed — instead focused on body geometry, joint articulation, and ease of assembly, so it can be posed realistically and rebuilt fast after an impact.

The dummy combines three material systems: EVA foam body panels for the outer shape, a PVC internal frame for stiffness and alignment, and 3D-printed PLA connectors that form the movable joints.

Most body sections move within a limited angle range; the neck and ankles stay fixed. That's enough to position the dummy in a believable human posture without over-complicating the structure.

3A.1 — REALIZATION · THREE PRODUCTION METHODS
01 LASER CUT

EVA foam panels

Panel shapes were laid out digitally, then laser-cut from a large foam mat. The challenge: forming flat panels into a 3D body shape, where small size or position errors change the final look.

02 FDM PRINT

PLA connectors

Joints and connectors were printed in PLA, oriented with supports for the complex shapes. Print tolerances meant parts needed manual checking and small fitting adjustments before assembly.

03 CUT + THREAD

PVC internal frame

PVC pipes define the body layout and support the foam panels. Foam was positioned around the frame and locked together by the printed joints — clearest in the leg section.

04 / The Bicycle

One big problem: the joints kept breaking.

The previous bicycle's 3D-printed joints weren't sturdy. The fix was a single principle change — and it touched all eleven joints.

✕ Previous design

Printed part inserts inside the tube

A thin, weak wall of plastic sat inside each PVC tube. On impact it fractured frequently — the joints were the failure point.

→
✓ Redesign

Printed part clamps over the tube

Wrapping the 19 mm tube outside-in carries load through a thicker, continuous wall. Internal stops prevent axial sliding; fillets cut stress and impact damage.

Bearings & a designed-to-break pin

The front (J2) and rear (J8) wheel joints each house a driving servo on one side and a bearing assembly on the other — so each wheel is supported yet free to spin, driven from a single side.

A shear-pin is the safety valve: dimensioned to fracture under excessive shear — like a vehicle impact — releasing the wheel rather than dumping the full load into the frame or the car.

The pin diameter was deliberately increased after the first iteration: small printed pins varied too much between prints. The larger stepped pin gives a repeatable fit while still failing in a controlled way.

4B.4 — SERVO DRIVE · THREE INDEPENDENT MOTORS
MOTOR 1

Front wheel

Drives the front wheel directly through its J2 housing — no gearbox in the path.

MOTOR 2

Rear wheel

Drives the rear wheel via J8, independently controllable in rotation and velocity.

MOTOR 3

Dummy pedalling

Actuates the mounted dummy, producing leg/pedal motion synced to the wheels.

Three independent servos replace a geared drivetrain entirely — cutting weight, simplifying maintenance, and giving each driven part its own rotation and velocity. The configuration was confirmed with the client and recommended by Jan Benders. Servo housings use a clearance fit with a rear access hole, after an early version trapped the servo too tightly.

JOINT ITERATION · SELECTED MILESTONES
J2 · v1 → v2

Servo that wouldn't come out

V1 tolerances were so tight the servo went in but not out — saws and pliers required. V2 fits cleanly and needs zero support.

J5 · ×4 breaks

Clamp force vs. release

The handlebar joint broke ~four times finding the right opening angle. The final version is smoothed, support-free, and stress-tested by repeated insertion.

J7 · in → over

Lower frame, re-walled

Switched from inside-tube to over-tube connections after seeing prior groups' parts fail. Fillets added to avoid scratching on impact.

J8 · v1 → v3

Bigger pins, more clearance

Breaking pins enlarged for print repeatability; servo clearance opened and a rear removal hole added.

05 / Electrical Design

One controller, two motor chains.

A central microcontroller drives a motor shield feeding two chains — two motors for the bike's wheels, four for the dummy's shoulders and hips — off a shared DC supply with separated motor and logic rails.

FIG 10Olimexino STM32F3
Fig 10 — Olimexino STM32F3: Arduino-compatible, with more headroom.
7A · CONTROLLER

Olimexino STM32F3

Chosen with the client for more computing power and accessibility than an Arduino — while staying Arduino-compatible, so an Arduino can be substituted if needed.

7B · SHIELD

OpenCM 485 EXP

The intermediary that lets the controller talk to the Dynamixel motors. Mounted via cables; legacy connector housings are best swapped for modern ones.

7C · SERVOS

Dynamixel motors

Client-selected for strong performance per euro — high torque for the price, plus a ready Dynamixel software library.

06 / Testing

Does it fit, and does it move?

Two checks on the dummy: can the EVA foam, PVC frame and PLA connectors be assembled as intended — and can the joints be posed into a realistic human stance.
